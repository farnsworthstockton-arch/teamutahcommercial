Due Diligence for Commercial Real Estate in Utah: The Complete Checklist

Purchasing commercial real estate is one of the most significant financial decisions an investor or business owner will make. Unlike residential transactions, commercial deals involve complex layers of analysis that can reveal critical issues affecting a property's value, usability, and legal standing. The due diligence period is your opportunity to verify every assumption and uncover every risk before your earnest money goes hard and the deal becomes binding.

This guide walks through the complete due diligence process for commercial property in Utah, including costs, timelines, and the state-specific issues that can trip up even experienced investors.

What Is Due Diligence?

Due diligence is the investigation and analysis period that occurs between the execution of a purchase agreement and the closing of a commercial real estate transaction. During this window, the buyer has the right to examine every aspect of the property and, depending on the contract terms, can withdraw from the deal if material issues are discovered.

In commercial real estate, due diligence is far more comprehensive than a standard home inspection. It encompasses environmental, legal, physical, financial, and regulatory analysis, each requiring specialized expertise and documentation.

The Due Diligence Timeline

Most commercial real estate purchase agreements in Utah provide a due diligence period of 30 to 60 days, though complex transactions involving land, environmental concerns, or government approvals may negotiate longer windows of 90 days or more.

Time management is critical during due diligence. Many of the required studies and reports have lead times of two to four weeks, so you need to initiate them immediately upon contract execution. A well-organized buyer will have Phase I environmental, title, and survey work ordered within the first 48 hours.

The due diligence period is not for casual exploration. It is a tightly managed process where delays in ordering reports can compress your analysis window and force decisions without complete information. Treat day one as the starting gun.

Environmental Assessments

Phase I Environmental Site Assessment

A Phase I ESA is the standard environmental investigation for any commercial property transaction. Conducted in accordance with ASTM E1527-21 standards and EPA All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI) requirements, the Phase I involves a site inspection, historical research, regulatory database review, and interviews to identify recognized environmental conditions (RECs) on the property.

A Phase I ESA does not involve soil or groundwater testing. Instead, it identifies whether there is reason to believe contamination may be present, which would trigger a Phase II investigation.

Typical cost: $2,500 to $5,000 depending on property size and complexity. Timeline: 2 to 4 weeks.

Phase II Environmental Site Assessment

If the Phase I identifies recognized environmental conditions, a Phase II ESA involves actual sampling and testing of soil, groundwater, or building materials (such as asbestos or lead paint). Phase II investigations are significantly more expensive and time-consuming, but they are essential when contamination is suspected.

In Utah, the Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) maintains databases of known contaminated sites and underground storage tanks that should be cross-referenced during environmental review. Properties near former mining operations, gas stations, dry cleaners, or industrial facilities warrant particular scrutiny.

Typical cost: $10,000 to $50,000+ depending on scope. Timeline: 4 to 8 weeks.

Legal and Title Review

Legal Due Diligence Checklist

  • Title commitment and title insurance policy review
  • ALTA/NSPS survey (current within 6 months)
  • Review all recorded easements, restrictions, and covenants
  • Verify legal description matches survey and deed
  • Check for pending litigation involving the property
  • Review all existing contracts (service, maintenance, management)
  • Confirm property tax status and any delinquencies
  • Verify entity ownership and authority to sell

Title review is one of the most critical elements of due diligence. The title commitment, issued by a title company, reveals the ownership history, all recorded liens, easements, encumbrances, and restrictions affecting the property. Any exceptions listed in the title commitment should be individually reviewed and understood before closing.

An ALTA/NSPS land title survey goes beyond a basic boundary survey to show improvements, easements, setbacks, encroachments, and access points. For any significant commercial acquisition, an ALTA survey is considered essential. The survey requirements should be specified using the optional Table A items relevant to your transaction.

Typical costs: Title insurance varies by purchase price (budget 0.5% to 1% of value). ALTA survey: $3,000 to $10,000 depending on property size and complexity.

Zoning and Permits

Confirming that the property's current use and your intended use comply with applicable zoning regulations is fundamental to due diligence. In Utah, zoning authority rests with municipal governments, so you need to verify the property's zoning designation directly with the relevant city or county planning department.

Key items to verify include:

  • Current zoning designation and all permitted uses
  • Any conditional use permits (CUPs) currently in effect
  • Setback, height, density, and parking requirements
  • Whether the property is a legal nonconforming use (grandfathered)
  • Any pending zoning changes or overlay districts affecting the area
  • Building permit history and certificate of occupancy status

Physical Inspection

A commercial property inspection is far more comprehensive than a residential inspection and should be conducted by a qualified commercial building inspector or engineering firm. The inspection should cover:

  • Structural systems: Foundation, framing, load-bearing walls, and overall structural integrity
  • Roof: Condition, remaining useful life, warranty status, and drainage
  • HVAC: Age, condition, capacity, and maintenance history of all heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems
  • Electrical: Service capacity, panel condition, code compliance, and adequacy for intended use
  • Plumbing: Supply and waste line condition, water heater capacity, and any evidence of leaks
  • ADA compliance: Accessibility of entrances, restrooms, parking, and common areas under the Americans with Disabilities Act
  • Fire and life safety: Sprinkler systems, fire alarms, egress, and fire department access
  • Parking and site: Pavement condition, drainage, lighting, and landscaping

The American Society of Home Inspectors (ASHI) and similar professional organizations maintain standards of practice for commercial inspections. However, for larger or more complex properties, engaging a commercial engineering firm that can provide detailed capital expenditure projections is advisable.

Typical cost: $3,000 to $15,000 depending on building size and complexity. Timeline: 1 to 2 weeks to schedule and complete.

Financial Analysis

Financial due diligence for income-producing commercial property involves verifying the revenue and expense assumptions that underpin the property's valuation. At minimum, you should obtain and analyze:

  • Three years of operating statements (income and expenses)
  • Current rent roll with lease expiration dates and renewal options
  • Property tax bills for the past three years
  • Insurance cost history
  • Utility bills for a full 12-month cycle
  • Capital expenditure history and upcoming needs
  • Any outstanding vendor contracts or service agreements

The CRE Finance Council recommends that buyers independently verify reported income through tenant estoppel certificates, which confirm each tenant's lease terms, rent amount, and any outstanding issues. Estoppels are particularly important for multi-tenant properties where the seller's representations about occupancy and rent must be validated directly with tenants.

Tenant and Lease Review

For properties with existing tenants, a thorough review of every lease is essential. Key items to examine include:

  • Lease term, commencement, and expiration dates
  • Base rent, rent escalations, and any rent abatements
  • Tenant improvement allowances and obligations
  • Operating expense pass-through structures (NNN, modified gross, full service)
  • Renewal options, expansion rights, and right of first refusal
  • Assignment and subletting provisions
  • Co-tenancy clauses and exclusive use restrictions
  • Personal guarantees and security deposits

Pay particular attention to any clauses that could restrict your ability to reposition the property, change its use, or modify common areas. Co-tenancy and exclusive use clauses can significantly limit future leasing flexibility.

Utah-Specific Considerations

Several due diligence items are unique to Utah or carry particular importance in the state's commercial real estate market:

Water Rights

In Utah, water rights are separate from land ownership and are governed by the Utah Division of Water Rights under the prior appropriation doctrine. If your intended use requires water access (irrigation, landscaping, manufacturing, or development), you must verify that adequate water rights are included with the property or can be acquired separately. Water right transfers require state approval and can take months to process.

Mineral Rights

Mineral rights in Utah may have been severed from the surface estate at some point in the property's history. A title search should reveal whether mineral rights convey with the property. In areas with oil, gas, or mining activity, reserved mineral rights can create access and surface disturbance issues that affect property value and usability.

Seismic Considerations

Utah's Wasatch Front sits along a major fault line, and seismic risk should be evaluated for any commercial property in the region. Older buildings may not meet current seismic building codes, and retrofit costs can be substantial. A seismic risk assessment may be advisable for properties built before 1975 or those with unreinforced masonry construction.

Special Assessment Districts

Many Utah municipalities use special assessment areas (SAAs) to fund infrastructure improvements. Buyers should verify whether the property is within any special assessment district and understand the ongoing assessment obligations, as these can represent a significant recurring cost that may not appear on standard operating statements.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Starting too late: Reports and inspections have lead times. Order everything within the first 48 hours of the due diligence period.
  • Relying on seller-provided information: Independently verify all financial data, occupancy claims, and property condition representations.
  • Ignoring deferred maintenance: Understand the capital expenditure requirements over the next 5 to 10 years and factor them into your acquisition pricing.
  • Overlooking environmental risk: Skipping a Phase I ESA to save money can expose you to cleanup liabilities that dwarf the property's value.
  • Not reading every lease: Buried lease provisions can restrict your ability to operate or reposition the property after closing.
  • Assuming zoning compliance: Verify current use legality directly with the municipality. Legal nonconforming status can be lost if the use is discontinued.
